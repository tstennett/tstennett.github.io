Introduction

Africa's horrible poverty rate and inadequate schooling system have an undeniable link which results in a cycle of poverty. This cycle is a primary factor in Africa's economic struggle as younger generations are inable to receive proper education which is required of higher-paying professions.

Expansion

As evident in the previous page, the African education system is terrible. The students are taught poorly, particularly in mathematics which is proven to be a deciding factor in middle-class acceptance. Additionally, the teachers treat their duties unprofessionally, resulting in less lectures for students. Overall, these factors result in the inability to receive higher-paying jobs, resulting in the youth entering or remaining in poverty for the remained of their adult life.

Secondly, as mentioned earlier, Africa is heavily dependant on physical labour. Despite frequent warnings, African parents already living in poverty have children to aid with the physical labour, providing more economic output. This is a short-term solution, as the children are incapable of having time to go to school, and are stuck with the same low-paying and unpredictable income for the rest of their live.

Therefore, Africa carries a burdensome poverty cycle which consists of the youth either being inable to attend school, or receiving horrid education, resulting in low-paying and unpredictable jobs for their adulthood. In their adulthood, due to the physical labour industries such as agriculture, those people may also have many kids who will share the same fate, resulting in the cycle repeating itself to more.
